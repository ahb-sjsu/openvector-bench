\begin{abstract}
Summary statistics of embedding geometry --- intrinsic dimension, effective rank,
hubness skew, ball-growth slope --- are routinely treated as properties of a corpus
and are widely believed to trade off against one another. We show that several of
these trade-offs are \emph{representational artifacts}: they appear when a
generative mechanism (or a learned representation) binds distinct geometric layers
to a single control, and they dissolve when the layers are given separate
mechanisms. We propose a four-layer decomposition of embedding geometry --- global
metric/spectral structure, corpus measure $\muC$, query measure $\muQ$, and local
scale structure --- and support it with a pre-registered falsification chain over a
procedural corpus-generator search (seven experiment rounds against a real
$1024$-dimensional text-embedding corpus). Three constructive separations result:
(i) angular hub structure vs.\ global spectrum, an apparently sharp Pareto
frontier dissolved by splitting placement from colouring; (ii) \emph{same hubness,
different anatomy}: a synthetic corpus and the real corpus share the same scalar
hubness reading produced by opposite mechanisms (centrality-driven super-hubs,
max reverse-$k$NN count 369, vs.\ density-driven moderate hubs, max count 78) ---
one-number hubness is non-identifying; (iii) hubness vs.\ ball growth, dissolved
by recognising that the real system's hubness lives substantially in the query
marginal: writing hubness as $H_k(\mathcal{C},\mathcal{Q},d)$, reverse-neighbour
counts sample the query mass of top-$k$ capture basins,
$N_k(x_j)\approx|\mathcal{Q}|\,\muQ(\capture{x_j})$. We complement the
empirical chain with an \emph{independent-realizability theorem}: the
achievable hub vectors of a fixed corpus form a polytope (the convex hull of
retrievable top-$k$ incidence vectors), and an arbitrarily small
simplex-address augmentation --- which preserves every corpus-to-corpus
neighbour ordering exactly and perturbs the spectrum by $O(\delta)$ ---
expands that polytope to the full hypersimplex $\Delta(n,k)$, so no universal
functional law of the form $G6=f(G2,G3,G7,G8)$ can hold over finite retrieval
experiments. We conclude that apparent geometric trade-offs can be artifacts
of forcing corpus metric, corpus measure, query measure, and local scale
through a shared mechanism, propose a hub \emph{anatomy vector} as the
reporting standard, and derive a registered anatomy falsifier that prevents
optimizers from reproducing a scalar by rebuilding a known pathology.

\end{abstract}

\section{Theory: the hubness polytope and the Bond independence theorem}
\label{sec:theory}

The clean result available here is an \emph{independent-realizability theorem},
not statistical independence. Define a retrieval experiment as the tuple
\[
\mathcal{E} = (X,\ \muC,\ \nuQ,\ d,\ k),
\]
where $X=\{x_1,\dots,x_n\}$ is the corpus, $\muC$ its measure, $\nuQ$ the query
measure, $d$ the metric, and $k$ the retrieval depth. Classical hubness uses
reverse-$k$NN occurrence counts \cite{radovanovic10}; order-$k$ Voronoi cells
partition query space by which $k$ corpus points are retrieved. Throughout,
assume queries in general position (ties $\nuQ$-null).

\subsection{The hubness polytope}

For $|S|=k$ define the order-$k$ cell
$V^{(k)}_S = \{q : \NN_k(q;X) = S\}$ and the normalized query-induced hub mass
$h_i(\nuQ) = \nuQ\{q : i \in \NN_k(q;X)\}$. Every query contributes exactly $k$
selections, so $0 \le h_i \le 1$ and $\sum_i h_i = k$.

\begin{theorem}[Hubness polytope]\label{thm:polytope}
For any fixed corpus and metric, the set of achievable hub vectors is
\[
\mathcal{H}_k(X,d) \;=\; \conv\bigl\{\mathbf{1}_S : V^{(k)}_S \neq \varnothing\bigr\},
\]
where $\mathbf{1}_S$ is the $0/1$ incidence vector of $S$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
For each query let $z(q) = \mathbf{1}_{\NN_k(q;X)}$; then
$h(\nuQ) = \int z(q)\,d\nuQ(q)$. Since $z$ takes only the incidence-vector
values of nonempty cells, its expectation lies in their convex hull.
Conversely, given $h = \sum_S p_S \mathbf{1}_S$, choose $q_S \in V^{(k)}_S$ for
each nonempty cell and set $\nuQ = \sum_S p_S\,\delta_{q_S}$.
\end{proof}

Query hubness is thus a \emph{linear image of the query measure} --- not
intrinsically a corpus statistic. Equation~\eqref{eq:capture} is the coordinate
form of this map.

\subsection{The simplex-address lemma: every hub vector becomes reachable}

Theorem~\ref{thm:polytope} is constrained by which cells happen to be nonempty.
An arbitrarily small augmentation removes even that constraint. Let
$Y = \{y_1,\dots,y_n\} \subset S^{d-1}$ be any unit-normalized corpus, and let
$v_1,\dots,v_n$ be the vertices of a regular simplex in $\mathbb{R}^{n-1}$
($v_i^\top v_i = 1$, $v_i^\top v_j = -\tfrac{1}{n-1}$ for $i \neq j$,
$\sum_i v_i = 0$). For $0 < \delta < 1$ construct the augmented corpus
\[
x_i = \bigl(\sqrt{1-\delta^2}\; y_i,\ \delta\, v_i\bigr) \in S^{d+n-2}.
\]

\begin{lemma}[Corpus anatomy is exactly preserved]\label{lem:preserve}
For $i \neq j$,
$x_i^\top x_j = (1-\delta^2)\, y_i^\top y_j - \tfrac{\delta^2}{n-1}$,
a strictly increasing affine map of the original cosine similarity. Hence every
corpus-to-corpus neighbour ordering, the base-to-base $k$NN graph, base-to-base
reverse-count hubness, and every rank-based local statistic are \emph{exactly}
preserved. For chord distance,
$\|x_i - x_j\|^2 = (1-\delta^2)\|y_i-y_j\|^2 + \tfrac{2n\delta^2}{n-1}$:
the full pairwise-distance matrix undergoes one common monotone
reparameterization.
\end{lemma}

\begin{lemma}[Every $k$-subset becomes retrievable]\label{lem:address}
For any $S$ with $|S| = k$, let $u_S = \sum_{i \in S} v_i$ and
$q_S = (0,\ u_S/\|u_S\|)$. Then $u_S^\top v_j = \tfrac{n-k}{n-1}$ for
$j \in S$ and $u_S^\top v_j = -\tfrac{k}{n-1}$ for $j \notin S$, so
$q_S^\top x_j$ is strictly larger for every $j \in S$ than for every
$j \notin S$, i.e.\ $\NN_k(q_S; X_\delta) = S$. Every $k$-subset has a
nonempty order-$k$ cell.
\end{lemma}

\begin{corollary}[Universal query-hubness control]\label{cor:hypersimplex}
For the augmented corpus $X_\delta$, the hubness polytope is the full
hypersimplex \cite{hypersimplex}:
\[
\mathcal{H}_k(X_\delta) \;=\; \Delta(n,k)
\;=\; \Bigl\{h \in [0,1]^n : \textstyle\sum_i h_i = k\Bigr\}.
\]
Every mathematically feasible query-induced hub vector is realizable exactly
(rational points exactly by finite query sets; arbitrary points to arbitrary
precision), while the complete corpus-to-corpus neighbour graph is unchanged.
\end{corollary}

\subsection{Spectral effect of the address block}

The address block's spectral footprint is controlled, though --- one correction
to the naive account --- not exactly diagonal. Writing the covariance of
$X_\delta$ in $(y,v)$ blocks: the diagonal blocks are $(1-\delta^2)\Cov(Y)$ and
$\tfrac{\delta^2}{n-1} I_{n-1}$ (the regular simplex satisfies
$\tfrac1n \sum_i v_i v_i^\top = \tfrac{1}{n-1} I$), so \emph{modulo the cross
term} the padded eigenvalues obey
\[
\lambda_i(X_\delta) \;=\; (1-\delta^2)\,\lambda_i(Y) + \frac{\delta^2}{n-1}
\quad\text{--- an explicit infinitesimal recolouring toward isotropy.}
\]
The cross-covariance
$\delta\sqrt{1-\delta^2}\,M$ with $M = \tfrac1n \sum_i y_i v_i^\top$ is
generically nonzero; by Weyl's inequality it perturbs each eigenvalue by at
most $2\delta\sqrt{1-\delta^2}\,\|M\|_2$, and $\|M\|_2 = O(n^{-1/2})$ for
generic $Y$. All spectral effects vanish as $\delta \to 0$, which is what
Theorem~\ref{thm:independence} uses. The separation is therefore unusually
strong: query hubness moves by $O(1)$; corpus neighbour anatomy moves by
exactly zero; spectral change is $O(\delta)$.

\subsection{Macro--micro scale separation}

It remains to separate the global spectrum from local ball growth. Choose macro
centres $c_a \in \mathbb{R}^p$ with minimum separation $\Delta > 0$, and for
each a centered local template
$U_a = \{u_{a1},\dots,u_{am_a}\} \subset \mathbb{R}^q$ with weights
$\beta_{a\ell}$, $\sum_\ell \beta_{a\ell} u_{a\ell} = 0$. Construct
$y_{a\ell} = (c_a,\ \epsilon\, u_{a\ell})$ with weight
$\alpha_a \beta_{a\ell}$.

\begin{lemma}[Spectrum and scale occupy orthogonal blocks]\label{lem:macromicro}
The covariance is exactly block diagonal:
\[
\Cov(Y_\epsilon) \;=\; \Cov(C) \,\oplus\, \epsilon^2 \sum_a \alpha_a \Cov(U_a),
\]
(the per-cluster centering kills the cross term identically). The $O(1)$
global spectrum is controlled by the macro centres; the microstructure enters
at $O(\epsilon^2)$. At radius $\epsilon r < \Delta$ no other cluster is
visible, and
$\muC\bigl(B(y_{a\ell}, \epsilon r)\bigr)
= \alpha_a \sum_{j:\|u_{aj}-u_{a\ell}\| \le r} \beta_{aj}$:
the local ball-growth curve is exactly the template's, rescaled in radius ---
precisely the degree of freedom behind ball-growth intrinsic-dimension
estimators \cite{multiscaleid}. Any target macro covariance
$\Sigma = \sum_{j=1}^r \lambda_j e_j e_j^\top$ is realizable by symmetric
centres $c_{j,\pm} = \pm\sqrt{r\lambda_j}\, e_j$ with weights $1/(2r)$.
\end{lemma}

\subsection{The Bond independence theorem}

\begin{theorem}[Universal approximate independence of spectrum, local scale,
and query hubness]\label{thm:independence}
Fix (i) a realizable target covariance spectrum $\Lambda$; (ii) finite target
local ball-growth templates; (iii) a retrieval depth $1 \le k < n$; (iv) any
feasible normalized hub vector $h \in \Delta(n,k)$; and (v) any tolerance
$\eta > 0$. Then in sufficiently high finite dimension there exist a corpus
$X$, corpus measure $\muC$, query measure $\nuQ$, and Euclidean or cosine
metric such that: $\|\lambda(\Cov X) - \Lambda\| < \eta$; the registered local
ball-growth profiles differ from their targets by less than $\eta$;
$h_i = \nuQ\{q : i \in \NN_k(q;X)\}$ holds exactly; and $\nuQ$ can be varied
throughout $\Delta(n,k)$ without modifying the corpus or any corpus-only
observable.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}[Proof construction]
(1)~Macro centres realize $\Lambda$ (Lemma~\ref{lem:macromicro}).
(2)~$\epsilon$-scale centered microtemplates realize the local growth curves.
(3)~Choose $\epsilon$ small enough that the microstructure moves the global
spectrum by less than $\eta/2$.
(4)~Append the $\delta$-scale regular-simplex address block
(Lemmas~\ref{lem:preserve}--\ref{lem:address}).
(5)~Choose $\delta \ll \epsilon$ so its spectral and radial effects (including
the Weyl cross-term bound) are below $\eta/2$.
(6)~Write $h$ as a convex combination of $k$-subset incidence vectors
(Corollary~\ref{cor:hypersimplex}).
(7)~Place the corresponding query mass on the simplex queries $q_S$.
\end{proof}

\paragraph{What this proves.} There is no universal functional law
$G6 = f(G2, G3, G7, G8)$ over all finite retrieval experiments: G6 query
hubness is controlled by the query measure over top-$k$ capture cells; G3/G8
by the macro covariance; G2 by microstructure and radius distribution; and
base-to-base hub anatomy can remain \emph{exactly} fixed while query-to-base
hubness moves arbitrarily. This is stronger than the empirical observation
that our knobs happened to separate.

\paragraph{What it does not prove.} The construction is existential and
deliberately permissive: the address block may require $n-1$ extra dimensions;
the query and corpus marginals may differ substantially; independence is not
established under $\nuQ = \muC$, nor in fixed dimension; nothing guarantees
the constructed corpora resemble natural embeddings; and semantic, encoder,
normalization, and shared-support restrictions can reintroduce coupling. The
proper claim: \emph{there is no universal geometric necessity coupling these
quantities --- any coupling observed in a restricted generator or a real
embedding family arises from additional structural constraints.} That is
exactly what rounds 5--7 exhibit empirically.

\subsection{A generator-specific controllability test (registered)}
\label{sec:jacobian}

The universal theorem says nothing about whether independence holds
\emph{inside a realistic family}. For the round-7 generator define the
intervention map
$F : (\theta_s, \theta_q, \theta_r) \mapsto (G3, G6, G2)$, where $\theta_s$ =
\texttt{spectrum\_decay}, $\theta_q$ = \texttt{query\_tail}, and $\theta_r$ =
the local-scale-homogeneity control (\texttt{equalize}; in this family G2's
empirical controller is window homogenization rather than a radius spectrum,
a fidelity note the experiment records). If the Jacobian
\[
J = \begin{pmatrix}
\partial G3/\partial\theta_s & \partial G3/\partial\theta_q & \partial G3/\partial\theta_r\\
\partial G6/\partial\theta_s & \partial G6/\partial\theta_q & \partial G6/\partial\theta_r\\
\partial G2/\partial\theta_s & \partial G2/\partial\theta_q & \partial G2/\partial\theta_r
\end{pmatrix}
\]
has full rank at the round-7 solution, the inverse-function theorem gives
\emph{local independent controllability} within the actual family.
Pre-registered expectations: $\theta_s$ dominates the G3 column, $\theta_q$
the G6 column, $\theta_r$ the G2 column; $J$ nonsingular with acceptable
condition number, stable across fresh seeds. \todo{Jacobian estimate at the

\section{Hubness is $H_k(\mathcal{C},\mathcal{Q},d)$: the capture-basin account}
\label{sec:hubness}
\begin{itemize}
\item Definition: $\capture{x_j}$ = the region of query space for which $x_j$
  ranks in the top $k$ under metric $d$ against corpus $\mathcal{C}$. Then
  \begin{equation}
  N_k(x_j) \;\approx\; |\mathcal{Q}| \cdot \muQ\!\left(\capture{x_j}\right).
  \label{eq:capture}
  \end{equation}
\item Corpus geometry defines the basins; the query marginal decides how
  heavily each is visited; identical skew can arise from huge basins
  (corpus-side pathology) or concentrated query mass (the real mechanism).
\item Consequences for the hubness literature: base$\to$base hubness
  (Radovanovi\'c-style, $H_k(\mathcal{C},d)$) and query-measured hubness are
  different observables and should not be conflated; benchmark suites that
  generate queries from the corpus distribution structurally cannot exhibit
  the query-induced regime. \todo{position against related work}
\end{itemize}

\section{Implications}\label{sec:implications}
\begin{itemize}
\item For benchmark design: corpus-only generators cannot reproduce
  query-induced statistics; workload models need an explicit $\muQ$.
\item For ANN evaluation: same-G6 corpora with opposite anatomy are natural
  differential tests (exact vs.\ HNSW/DiskANN/FAISS/cuVS; fp32 vs.\ quantized;
  metric choice; build order; sharding), with oracles on hub-rank divergence
  and anti-hub-query recall collapse --- aggregate recall hides exactly the
  tail the decomposition exposes. Companion paper: \emph{Fuzzing the Bond
  Metric} (coverage signature over layer-separated cells).
\item For representation learning: apparent statistic trade-offs in learned
  embeddings deserve the same suspicion --- they may be facts about the
  architecture's control structure, not about geometry.
\end{itemize}

\section{Limitations and the road to a theorem}\label{sec:limits}
\begin{itemize}
\item Corroboration, not universal confirmation: three separations on one real
  corpus family (text embeddings, one model class) and one generator lineage.
\item What a theorem needs: a formal generative class in which the four layers
  are independently parameterized, plus proofs that each gate is invariant to
  the other layers' controls within stated ranges; candidate assumptions
  (unit-sphere metric, sub-Gaussian patches, capture-basin regularity).
\item Known couplings that are real, not representational: the $s^{-d_\ell}$
  amplification; normalization's coupling of radial to angular structure;
  where the decomposition should be expected to fail.
\end{itemize}
